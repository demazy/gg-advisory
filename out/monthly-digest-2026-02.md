**Signals Digest — 2026-02**

**Top Lines**
- Australia’s electricity consumption in the NEM is forecast to nearly double by 2050.
- The annualised capital cost for energy infrastructure is estimated at $128 billion by 2050.
- From 1 May 2026, changes to STC eligibility for solar batteries will take effect.

## Energy Transition
**Energy security: Keeping the lights on through the energy transition (QLD)**  
PUBLISHED: 2026-02-26  
Summary: Rainer Korte, Commissioner at AEMC, discussed the ongoing energy transition in Australia, emphasizing the need for a fivefold increase in grid-scale wind and solar by 2050. The Draft 2026 Integrated System Plan (ISP) indicates that electricity consumption in the National Electricity Market (NEM) is expected to nearly double by 2050, necessitating significant investments. The estimated annual capital cost for utility-scale generation, storage, transmission, and distribution infrastructure is $128 billion by 2050.  
Why it matters: CFOs at energy-intensive companies should prepare for increased electricity demand and consider investment in renewable energy sources to mitigate future costs.

**Energy security: Keeping the lights on through the energy transition (NSW)**  
PUBLISHED: 2026-02-24  
Summary: In a similar address, Rainer Korte highlighted the critical need for maintaining system security as coal plants retire. The AEMO's Transition Plan for System Security identifies gaps that require coordinated action over the next decade. The retirement of Eraring Power Station is now extended to 30 April 2029, allowing more time for new infrastructure to be developed.  
Why it matters: Boards should assess their reliance on coal and plan for alternative energy sources to ensure operational continuity and compliance with upcoming regulatory changes.

**Carbon Abatement Schemes Deliver Emissions Reductions Comparable To Year Road Transport**  
PUBLISHED: 2026-02-01  
Summary: The Clean Energy Regulator reported that carbon abatement schemes reduced emissions by approximately 83.7 million tonnes of CO2-e in 2025, equivalent to removing all cars and trucks from the road for a year. The uptake of home batteries surged, with over 193,000 installations providing 4.6 GWh of capacity.  
Why it matters: Investors in renewable energy should monitor the growth of carbon credit schemes and consider the implications for compliance and investment strategies.

## ESG Reporting
**ISSB Update February 2026**  
PUBLISHED: 2026-02-01  
Summary: The ISSB met on 25 February 2026 and made preliminary decisions regarding the IFRS Sustainability Disclosure Standards. Amendments to the IFRS S2 industry-based guidance are expected to be proposed in March 2026, with an effective date set for 12–18 months post-issuance.  
Why it matters: CFOs must prepare for the upcoming changes in sustainability reporting standards, ensuring compliance with new disclosure requirements.

**EFRAG Calls for Balanced and Cost-Effective Approach to Proposed GHG Protocol Scope 2 Changes**  
PUBLISHED: 2026-02-20  
Summary: EFRAG submitted its position on proposed changes to the GHG Protocol Scope 2 Guidance, advocating for clarity and proportionality in the revisions. EFRAG emphasizes the need for a careful cost-benefit analysis and adequate consultation periods to ensure effective implementation.  
Why it matters: Companies should engage with the GHG Protocol updates to align their emissions reporting with evolving standards and avoid potential compliance risks.

## Sustainable Finance & Investment
**Statement on Monetary Policy**  
PUBLISHED: 2026-02-03  
Summary: The Reserve Bank of Australia released its Statement on Monetary Policy, assessing current economic conditions and risks. The report is crucial for informing the Board's monetary policy decisions and is published quarterly.  
Why it matters: Institutional investors should closely monitor the RBA's assessments to gauge economic trends that could impact investment strategies and risk management.

**Changes To Rebate Solar Batteries 1 May**  
PUBLISHED: 2026-02-17  
Summary: Starting 1 May 2026, amendments to the Renewable Energy (Electricity) Regulations 2001 will adjust the number of small-scale technology certificates (STCs) solar batteries are eligible for. The STC factor will decline more frequently and at a higher rate, impacting the financial viability of solar battery installations.  
Why it matters: Businesses considering solar battery investments must evaluate the financial implications of the upcoming STC changes and adjust their strategies accordingly.
